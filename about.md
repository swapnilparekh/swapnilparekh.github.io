I'm Swapnil Parekh — an AI Scientist at Intuit, where I work on the systems that power Intuit Assist: multi-agentic LLM infrastructure, retrieval-augmented generation, and the alignment work that makes large models useful in high-stakes financial contexts. Before Intuit, I was at IBM Research on the core team for watsonx.ai's LLM inference engine, and before that I earned an MS in Computer Engineering from NYU. I also occasionally compete in Kaggle (Expert), and I shipped the first MCP implementation at Intuit as part of the Intuit × Anthropic partnership.

My research sits at the intersection of mechanistic interpretability, adversarial robustness, and agentic systems. I want to understand *why* neural networks behave the way they do — and what happens when that behavior is deliberately subverted. I have 80+ citations and papers at AAAI, Springer FICC, Dialogue & Discourse, and CIKM.

## Research Interests

- **Mechanistic Interpretability** — Circuit-level analysis of transformer models; understanding which components encode which behaviors, and how to make that analysis reproducible and uncertainty-aware (CIRCUS).
- **Adversarial Robustness** — Universal adversarial triggers, attack transferability under model compression, and multimodal safety. I'm interested in the gap between empirical robustness claims and real-world deployment conditions.
- **Agentic & Latent Reasoning Systems** — Backdoor attacks on chain-of-thought and latent reasoning models; steering and safety for systems that reason in ways we can't directly observe.

## On Music

Outside of research I spend a lot of time listening to classical music. There is something about the structural logic of a well-constructed fugue or string quartet — the way themes transform and return changed — that rhymes with how I think about neural circuits. Some pieces I keep coming back to:

| Piece | Why it gets me |
|-------|---------------|
| Schubert — String Quartet No. 14 in D minor, D. 810 *"Death and the Maiden"* | The second movement is one of the most perfect things ever written. Slow, inexorable, devastating. |
| Bach — Cello Suites (Yo-Yo Ma, 1983 recording) | Pure structure. No harmony, just a single voice implying everything. |
| Shostakovich — String Quartet No. 8 in C minor, Op. 110 | Written in three days. Autobiography as music. The DSCH motif reappearing like a signature on a confession. |
| Beethoven — String Quartet No. 14 in C# minor, Op. 131 | Seven movements played without pause. The only piece I know that makes time feel different. |
| Arvo Pärt — *Spiegel im Spiegel* | The title means "Mirror in the Mirror." Infinite reflection. Useful when debugging at 2am. |
